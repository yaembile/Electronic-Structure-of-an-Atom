Learning Target

I can explain how the quantum mechanical model developed, write electron configurations using quantum mechanical principles, and predict main energy levels, sublevels, and orbitals in relation to the electronic configuration of elements.

Core idea: An electron's address is written from big to small: shell (n) → sublevel (ℓ) → orbital orientation (mℓ) → spin (ms).
Competency 1

Cheatsheet: Development of the Quantum Mechanical Model

From Fixed Paths to Probability

Bohr pictured electrons in fixed circular shells. The quantum mechanical model says electrons are found in probability regions called orbitals.

Wave-Particle Idea

Electrons act like particles and waves. Because of this, we describe where an electron is likely to be, not an exact path.

Orbitals Replace Orbits

An orbit is a path. An orbital is a region of high probability. Quantum numbers describe each orbital and electron.

Simple story: Old atomic models tried to draw electron roads. The quantum model gives a map of likely rooms where electron guests can be found.

Competency 2

Cheatsheet: Writing Electronic Configurations

1. Follow Energy Order

Use the Aufbau order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s...

2. Respect Room Capacity

s holds 2, p holds 6, d holds 10, and f holds 14 electrons.

3. Check the Total

For a neutral atom, the superscripts must add up to the atomic number.

Example: Magnesium has 12 electrons: 1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2. The superscripts add to 12.
Why the cheatsheet rules work: The filling order follows energy, not just floor number. Some rooms on a higher floor, like 4s, are slightly lower in energy than some rooms on a lower floor, like 3d. The capacity rules come from the number of orbital rooms in each sublevel, and the spin rule comes from Pauli: one room can hold only two opposite-spin electrons.

I Do: Full and Condensed Configuration

Sample: Sodium, Na, has 11 electrons.

Full: 1s2 2s2 2p6 3s1

Condensed: [Ne] 3s1

The noble-gas core is [Ne] because the first 10 electrons match neon. The remaining electron is in the valence shell, n = 3.

Competency 3

Cheatsheet: Energy Levels, Sublevels, and Orbitals

n Principal Quantum Number

Means: main energy level or shell.

Allowed: 1, 2, 3, 4, ...

Predicts: energy, size, distance from nucleus.

Memory cue: n is the floor number in an electron hotel.

ℓ Angular Momentum

Means: sublevel or orbital shape.

Allowed: 0 to n - 1.

Codes: 0=s, 1=p, 2=d, 3=f.

Memory cue: ℓ chooses the room type.

mℓ Magnetic Quantum Number

Means: orbital orientation in space.

Allowed: -ℓ to +ℓ, including 0.

Count: 2ℓ + 1 orbitals in a sublevel.

Memory cue: mℓ chooses the exact room.

ms Spin Quantum Number

Means: electron spin inside one orbital.

Allowed: +1/2 or -1/2 only.

Capacity: each orbital holds 2 electrons.

Memory cue: two opposite-spin seats per room.

The Atomic Hotel for Electrons

Imagine the atom as a hotel. The positively charged nucleus is the basement lobby and the electrons are guests assigned to rooms. Because electrons are negatively charged, they are attracted to the positive nucleus, so every guest feels a pull toward the basement.

Each floor is a main energy level, n. Each subshell is a room type, ℓ. Each room is an orbital, mℓ. Each bed direction is spin, ms. The full quantum-number address tells exactly where an electron guest belongs.

Best analogy: lower floors are closer to the positive nucleus, so electrons there are held more tightly and have lower energy. Higher floors are farther away, feel less attraction, and usually have higher energy.
Why this makes sense: Opposite charges attract. The positive nucleus attracts negative electrons. An electron on n = 1 is very close to the nucleus, so it is strongly attracted and more stable. An electron on a higher n floor is farther away, feels less pull, and is easier to remove or excite.
Manager Aufbau: Fill the cheapest rooms first. Aufbau sends guests to the lowest-energy available rooms before opening higher-energy rooms. This is why configurations begin with 1s, then 2s, then 2p, and so on.
Manager Pauli: Two guests per room, opposite seats. Pauli allows only two electrons in one orbital room, and they must sit in opposite spin seats: +1/2 and -1/2.
Manager Hund: Spread out before pairing. Hund tells electron guests in equal-energy rooms, like the three p rooms, to occupy separate rooms first before sharing.
Why learn the quantum-number address? The address tells us exactly where an electron belongs: floor (n), room type (ℓ), exact room (mℓ), and spin seat (ms). That address helps us write electron configurations, draw orbital diagrams, predict valence electrons, explain magnetism, and understand how atoms bond.

Worked Example 1: 3p

Question: Give n, ℓ, mℓ, number of orbitals, and max electrons for 3p.

n = 3 because the number is 3.

ℓ = 1 because p means 1.

mℓ = -1, 0, +1, so there are 3 orbitals.

Max electrons = 6 because 3 orbitals x 2 electrons each.

Worked Example 2: Element Configuration

Element: Calcium, atomic number 20.

Configuration: 1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p6 4s2

Last occupied sublevel: 4s.

Predictions: n = 4, ℓ = 0, mℓ = 0, one 4s orbital, max 2 electrons.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. ℓ cannot equal n

If n = 2, allowed ℓ values are 0 and 1 only.

2. ms is only spin

ms must be +1/2 or -1/2. It is never 0, 1, or 2.

3. One orbital holds two

A sublevel may contain many orbitals, but each single orbital holds only 2 electrons.
